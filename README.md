## Project Background

### What is RFM Analysis?

RFM is a customer segmentation framework used by CRM and marketing teams to rank customers based on three behavioural dimensions:

**Recency** — how recently a customer made a purchase. A customer who bought last week is more likely to respond to a campaign than one who bought two years ago.

**Frequency** — how many times a customer has purchased. Repeat buyers are more loyal and more predictable than one-time buyers.

**Monetary** — how much a customer has spent in total. High-spend customers represent disproportionate revenue and deserve disproportionate retention effort.

Together, these three dimensions place every customer into a segment that tells the marketing team what to do next: reward, re-engage, win back, or suppress.

### Dataset

The analysis uses the **UCI Online Retail dataset**, a publicly available transactional dataset from a UK-based gift and homeware retailer. The dataset covers December 2010 to December 2011 and contains 541,909 transaction rows across 38 countries.

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| InvoiceNo | Unique order identifier |
| StockCode | Product identifier |
| Description | Product name |
| Quantity | Units purchased per line |
| InvoiceDate | Date and time of transaction |
| UnitPrice | Price per unit in GBP |
| CustomerID | Unique customer identifier |
| Country | Customer country |

After cleaning, the working dataset contains **397,924 transaction rows** across **4,337 unique customers**, representing 73.5% of the raw data. Removed records include NULL customer IDs (135,080 rows), cancellations identified by invoice prefix C (9,288 rows), negative or zero unit prices, and non-product stock codes such as POST and BANK CHARGES.

### Goal

> "Which customers are our best, which are slipping, and which are already gone? And what should the CRM team do about each?"

The analysis produces a scored, segmented customer table in PostgreSQL that Power BI reads directly via live connection, eliminating any CSV export step from the pipeline.

## North-Star Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Champions Revenue Share** | Revenue generated by Champions as % of total | 48.4% |
| **At Risk Revenue** | Total spend of customers who used to buy frequently but have gone inactive | £895,642 |
| **Overall Customer Value** | Average total spend per customer across the dataset | £2,043 |
| **Revenue Concentration Ratio** | % of customers driving 80% of total revenue | Top 26.2% |
| **Average Recency** | Mean days since last purchase across all customers | 93 days |

### Why these five?

**Champions Revenue Share** is the headline number. It tells the business how dependent it is on a small group of customers and quantifies the risk of losing any of them.

**At Risk Revenue** is the recovery opportunity. These are customers who have already demonstrated willingness to spend but have stopped. Unlike acquiring new customers, winning them back has a known baseline of past behaviour to target.

**Overall Customer Value** sets the floor for what a reactivated customer is worth. Any win-back campaign costing less than £2,043 per recovered customer is ROI positive if it succeeds.

**Revenue Concentration Ratio** quantifies the Pareto imbalance. Knowing that 26.2% of customers drive 80% of revenue tells the CRM team exactly where to concentrate retention budget.

**Average Recency** at 93 days is the health baseline. Any segment with avg recency significantly above 93 is drifting toward churn.

## Executive Summary

The UCI Online Retail dataset contains 4,337 customers who generated **£8,860,738 in total revenue** between December 2010 and December 2011. RFM scoring was performed entirely in PostgreSQL using NTILE(5) window functions to assign scores of 1 to 5 across Recency, Frequency, and Monetary dimensions. Customers were then segmented into 10 groups using CASE WHEN logic based on score combinations.

The central finding is a severe revenue concentration problem. **Champions represent 13.1% of customers but generate 48.4% of total revenue** (£4,290,365). The top 26.2% of customers by spend account for 80% of all revenue. This concentration means the business is structurally dependent on fewer than 1,200 customers, and losing a meaningful portion of Champions would have an outsized financial impact.

The most actionable finding is the At Risk segment. **655 customers (15.1% of the base) have generated £895,642 historically but have not purchased in an average of 148 days.** These customers once bought 3.4 times on average, demonstrating genuine purchase intent. They are not lost yet, but their recency score of 1.7 indicates they are trending toward the Lost segment. A targeted win-back campaign has a defined revenue ceiling of £895,642 and a known customer profile to target.

At the other end of the spectrum, 685 customers across the Hibernating (328) and Lost (357) segments have average recency of 272 and 281 days respectively. These customers have not purchased in over nine months, have average order frequencies below 1.1, and collectively represent only 2.8% of total revenue. The cost of re-engaging them is unlikely to be justified by the expected return.

Geographic concentration compounds the revenue risk. The United Kingdom accounts for 3,919 of 4,337 customers and £7,284,795 of £8,860,738 in revenue. The remaining revenue is distributed across 37 countries with no single market contributing more than £285,446. The business is essentially a single-market operation with marginal international presence.

## Insight Deep Dive

### 1. Revenue Concentration: The Pareto Finding

**Observation**

The top 26.2% of customers by spend generate 80% of total revenue. Champions, who represent 13.1% of the customer base, alone account for 48.4% of revenue at £4,290,365.

| Segment | Customers | % of Base | Revenue | % of Revenue | Avg Spend |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Champions | 569 | 13.1% | £4,290,365 | 48.4% | £7,540 |
| Loyal Customers | 828 | 19.1% | £2,343,451 | 26.4% | £2,830 |
| At Risk | 655 | 15.1% | £895,642 | 10.1% | £1,367 |
| All others | 2,285 | 52.7% | £1,331,280 | 15.1% | £583 |

**So what?**

The bottom 52.7% of customers generate only 15.1% of revenue. From a pure resource allocation standpoint, every pound spent retaining a Champion is worth roughly 13 times the same pound spent on a Hibernating customer. The CRM team should not distribute retention budget evenly across segments.

**Why this pattern exists**

This is a B2B-adjacent retail pattern. Many of this retailer's high-frequency, high-spend customers are small businesses buying wholesale for resale rather than individual consumers. These buyers place large, repeat orders and drive the concentration. The average Champion has ordered 13.3 times and spent £7,540, which is not consumer retail behaviour.

**Recommendation**

Protect Champions before anything else. Introduce a tiered loyalty programme with early access to new stock, a dedicated account contact, and volume pricing. Champions buying 13.3 times per year should never need to reach a general customer service queue.

### 2. At Risk Segment: The Highest-Leverage Recovery Opportunity

**Observation**

655 customers are classified At Risk. They have an average of 3.4 past orders and average historical spend of £1,367, but have not purchased in an average of 148 days. Their RFM profile shows high Frequency scores (avg 3.4) combined with low Recency scores (avg 1.7), confirming they were once active buyers who have gone quiet.

```
At Risk profile:
Customers:         655
Total revenue:     £895,642
Avg spend:         £1,367
Avg days inactive: 148 days
Avg past orders:   3.4
```

**So what?**

£895,642 is sitting in customers who have already demonstrated purchase intent. This is not speculative revenue from new customer acquisition, it is historical spend from customers the business has already paid to acquire. The break-even recovery rate is low. If a win-back campaign costs £50 per customer (£32,750 total) and converts even 5% of At Risk customers (33 customers) at their historical average spend of £1,367, the campaign generates £45,111 against £32,750 in cost.

**Why they went quiet**

At Risk customers with 3.4 average orders are not one-time buyers who never intended to return. The most common causes are a competitor capturing their repeat business, a poor last experience that was never resolved, or simple recency decay where the brand dropped out of mind. The 148-day average inactive period is recent enough that a personalised re-engagement can still land before the customer considers themselves churned.

**Recommendation**

Run a segmented win-back sequence within 30 days. Email 1 on day 1: "We noticed you have not visited recently, here is what is new in the categories you bought from." Email 2 on day 7: show their purchase history and recommend complementary products. Email 3 on day 14: time-limited offer of 10-15% on their most purchased category. Track reactivation rate (next purchase within 60 days) as the campaign KPI.

### 3. Customer Health Distribution

**Observation**

Mapping all 10 segments by average recency reveals a clear split between active and inactive customer groups:

| Health Status | Segments | Customers | Avg Recency | Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Active | Champions, Loyal, Potential Loyalists, New Customers | 2,017 | 13 days | £7,188,337 |
| Slipping | At Risk, Need Attention, About to Sleep | 1,251 | 106 days | £1,238,250 |
| Inactive | Hibernating, Lost | 685 | 277 days | £286,655 |

46.5% of customers are active, 28.8% are slipping, and 15.8% are effectively inactive. The slipping group represents the most urgent intervention target, the inactive group represents a suppression candidate.

**The About to Sleep finding**

396 customers average 118 days inactive with average spend of only £408. These customers bought infrequently (avg 1.1 orders) and spent little. The combination of low historical value and growing recency makes them expensive to reactivate relative to expected return. They should receive at most one low-cost re-engagement attempt before being moved to suppressed status.

**New Customers need immediate nurturing**

137 New Customers average only 19 days since first purchase and £366 in spend. These customers are in the critical window where first-purchase experience determines whether they become Potential Loyalists or churn after one order. An onboarding sequence in the first 30 days, category discovery emails, and a second-purchase incentive should activate automatically for all new customers.

**Recommendation**

Treat the three health groups as three separate budget pools. Active customers get retention and upsell investment. Slipping customers get win-back campaigns with defined ROI thresholds. Inactive customers get one suppression email asking for feedback, then removed from active marketing lists to reduce cost.

### 4. Geographic Concentration

**Observation**

| Country | Customers | Revenue | Avg Spend |
|---|---|---|---|
| United Kingdom | 3,919 | £7,284,795 | £1,859 |
| Netherlands | 9 | £285,446 | £31,716 |
| EIRE | 3 | £262,172 | £87,391 |
| Germany | 94 | £228,867 | £2,434 |
| France | 87 | £199,566 | £2,294 |

The United Kingdom accounts for 90.4% of customers and 82.2% of revenue. However the Netherlands and EIRE figures are striking. Nine Netherlands customers generate £285,446 at an average spend of £31,716 per customer, and three EIRE customers generate £262,172 at £87,391 average. These are clearly wholesale or trade buyers, not retail consumers.

**So what?**

The Netherlands and EIRE customers are individually the highest-value in the dataset by a wide margin. Nine customers generating £285,446 means each one is worth approximately 17 Champion-tier UK customers. Losing one of these accounts would be felt immediately in the revenue line. They warrant personal account management regardless of what segment the RFM model assigns them.

**Recommendation**

Flag all non-UK customers with spend above £10,000 for manual account review. These are trade relationships, not retail customers, and should not be managed by automated CRM campaigns. Assign a dedicated account manager and schedule quarterly check-ins.

### 5. Segment Behaviour Profiles

**Observation**

The RFM scores reveal distinct behavioural fingerprints across segments:

| Segment | Avg R Score | Avg F Score | Avg M Score | Avg Orders | Avg Recency |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Champions | 5.0 | 4.8 | 4.7 | 13.3 | 6 days |
| Loyal Customers | 3.7 | 4.5 | 4.2 | 6.5 | 32 days |
| At Risk | 1.7 | 3.6 | 3.4 | 3.4 | 148 days |
| Potential Loyalists | 4.4 | 2.6 | 2.6 | 2.0 | 17 days |
| Need Attention | 3.0 | 3.0 | 3.1 | 2.3 | 51 days |
| About to Sleep | 2.0 | 1.8 | 1.9 | 1.1 | 118 days |
| Hibernating | 1.0 | 1.5 | 1.6 | 1.2 | 272 days |
| Lost | 1.0 | 1.0 | 1.0 | 1.0 | 281 days |

**The Potential Loyalists opportunity**

483 Potential Loyalists have a Recency score of 4.4, meaning they bought very recently, but Frequency of only 2.0, meaning they have bought twice on average. These customers are in the exact window where a well-timed second or third purchase nudge can shift them toward the Loyal Customers segment. Their average spend of £1,044 across only 2 orders suggests significant upside if frequency increases.

If even 20% of Potential Loyalists (97 customers) increase their order frequency to match Loyal Customer averages, the expected revenue uplift is approximately 97 customers x (£2,830 minus £1,044) = £173,242 in incremental revenue from existing customers with no acquisition cost.

**Lost vs Hibernating**

Lost (281 days, 1.0 avg orders) and Hibernating (272 days, 1.2 avg orders) are nearly identical in recency but Lost customers have the absolute minimum scores across all three dimensions. Neither group justifies significant marketing spend. The combined revenue of both segments is £286,655 across 685 customers, and the reactivation rate for customers inactive for 9+ months in retail is typically below 5%.

## Technical Approach

**Pipeline: PostgreSQL → Power BI**

All data ingestion, cleaning, and RFM scoring was performed in PostgreSQL. Raw data was loaded using the PostgreSQL COPY command rather than pandas, reflecting production data engineering practice.

**SQL concepts used:**
- COPY command for bulk data ingestion
- CTEs (WITH clauses) for multi-step RFM calculation
- NTILE(5) window function for percentile-based scoring
- SUM() OVER() and COUNT() OVER() for percentage calculations
- MODE() WITHIN GROUP for multi-value country deduplication
- PostgreSQL regex (stock_code ~ '^[0-9]') for non-product filtering
- CREATE VIEW for live Power BI connection without CSV export

**Power BI** connects directly to PostgreSQL views via the native connector in Import mode. No CSV files were generated at any stage of the pipeline.

```
Stack: PostgreSQL 15 | Power BI Desktop
Dataset: UCI Online Retail | 541,909 raw rows | 4,337 customers | Dec 2010 to Dec 2011
```
